## Reason for Request

At present, the Apple Watch benefits from access to the WatchConnectivity framework, which
enables deep integration and provides, among other things, interactive messaging,
background transfers, file transfers and complication updates. Third-party developers can
create iOS apps and rely on WatchConnectivity to support communication with their respective
Apple Watch apps (like Strava, Spotify, or a fitness tracker). The WatchConnectivity framework
allows these companion apps and WatchOS apps to sync data, send commands and update
displays even if the iOS app is in the background or the iPhone is locked, because the
WatchConnectivity framework is able to queue messages and deliver when both devices are
ready and can route over Bluetooth, Wi-Fi or cellular connectivity. As we understand from your
documentation, the transfer of the data is controlled by a system-level software stack: “After
initiating a transfer from your app, the system assumes responsibility for the transmission of
any data.”; “The system sends context data when the opportunity arises, with the goal of
having the data ready to use by the time the counterpart wakes up. ”; “If an app performs
another transfer before finishing the previous one, the system queues the transfers and delivers
them in the order received.”
However, the WatchConnectivity framework is only available for iOS apps communicating with
watchOS apps on Apple Watch, not for third-party wearable devices. Third-party wearable
devices are not able to participate in Apple’s wearables ecosystem due to the restriction
against access to these key APIs. This means that, to attempt to replicate (in lower quality) the
behaviour that the Apple Watch enjoys, either a third-party wearable device manufacturer must
make available an SDK to be integrated by each developer that wishes to have its iOS app
communicate effectively with the third-party wearable device, or each app developer/wearable
developer must create new APIs for integration with the third-party wearable device.
We believe that access to the WatchConnectivity framework is the easiest way of closing the
interoperability gap between what Apple makes available to the Apple Watch and what is
available to third-party wearable devices. If developers could develop their apps once and
provide access to wearable devices from several manufacturers, we would not have to require
those developers to adopt yet another API, and they could benefit from the central handling of
multiple wearables, which ultimately would be better for the system and consumers.
The current lack of this functionality leads to:
- Oversized app binaries;
- Fragmentation on the phone (as there can end up being multiple versions of a
framework); and
- A lack of centralized management (given apps cannot efficiently talk to each other, nor
do they know system state, leading to poor management of connectivity and data
queuing. By contrast, WatchConnectivity framework is the single point that all apps will
interface with and has near perfect context).
Therefore, Meta would like Apple to grant its wearables’ companion app access to use the
WatchConnectivity framework in order to communicate with its wearables. More specifically,
we would like to be able to build a wearable that would participate in the same ecosystem as
the Apple Watch by accessing the wearable side of WatchConnectivity, for example.
We have evaluated technologies such as Core Bluetooth and iAP protocols, but those do not
benefit from central coordination like the WatchConnectivity APIs do. Moreover, since we do
not have inter-process communication between apps in the same way Apple services and
frameworks do, and we do not have adequate access to System Status information, therefore
we cannot build an equivalent and performant API.
Finally, to be clear, our request is not subsumed by the measures specified by the European
Commission’s decision in the specification proceedings Case DMA.100203. While that decision
covers some functionalities relevant to our request (e.g., “Automatic Wi-Fi Connection” and
“Background Execution”), the decision covers only a limited subset of the functionality we have
requested, which includes a broader ability for third-party wearables to communicate with apps
on users’ iPhones. We therefore hereby request interoperability with those functionalities which
support the features mentioned above and which are not encompassed by the European
Commission’s decision.
We are happy to provide any clarification you may require.

April 2, 2026, from Apple to Developer:
Thank you for your interoperability request.
Your request contains a generic description of a wide range of capabilities of the
WatchConnectivity framework, including “interactive messaging, background transfers, file
transfers and complication updates”, the ability to “queue messages and deliver when both
devices are ready,” and routing “over Bluetooth, Wi-Fi or cellular connectivity”. Your request
does not however identify any specific capabilities of iOS that you currently lack access to. You
have also listed “Multipeer Connectivity” in the title of your request but have not explained
which specific capabilities of iOS you are seeking to access in this context.
A number of the concerns you raised in your request, including with regard to “oversized app
binaries” and “fragmentation on the phone (as there can end up being multiple versions of a
framework)” do not relate to access to iOS features within the meaning of Article 6(7) DMA and
are therefore outside the scope of our interoperability process.
We have assessed your request based on our understanding of the general capabilities which
you appear to be seeking as best we can understand them. Irrespective of whether
WatchConnectivity is even in scope of the DMA, we believe it very likely that our ongoing work
pursuant to the European Commission’s specification proceedings in case DMA.100203
overlaps with some or all of the key areas you have identified. We are therefore moving your
request to Phase 3 of our interoperability process. We expect to proceed on the timeframes set
by the European Commission in its specification decision and we are happy to review any
further nuances with you once the implementation is available in a seed build. Please note that
Apple’s implementation of the Commission’s specification decision will exhaust Apple’s
obligations in relation to the functionalities covered by that decision.
This concludes our review under Article 6(7) DMA.
If you have additional questions, you may reach out to us through Apple Developer Forums
(https://developer.apple.com/forums/), Feedback Assistant (https://developer.apple.com/bug-
reporting/), or a code-level support request (https://developer.apple.com/contact/request/
code-level-support/).
April 8, 2026, from Developer to Apple:
Dear Apple Developer Relations Team,
Thank you for your response to our interoperability request (FB22139833). We take note of your
statement that this request is being moved to Phase 3 on the basis of its potential overlap with
Apple’s ongoing implementation of the European Commission's (EC) specification decision in
case DMA.100203.
We do, however, take issue with your characterization of our request as “generic.” This request
(originally INTEROP-171) has been the subject of technical dialogue since March 2024. In May
2024, we explicitly identified the frameworks at issue and explained why alternative tools—
specifically CoreBluetooth and iAP—are insufficient.
To ensure there is no further ambiguity regarding the specific iOS capabilities we seek, we
provide the following technical clarifications:
1. Core Object: System-Level Orchestration and “Fair Treatment”
Meta seeks access to the centralized orchestration layer provided by the WatchConnectivity
and Multipeer Connectivity services to ensure non-discriminatory access for the third-party
wearable ecosystem. This would enable:
-App-to-Device Interoperability: Any iOS app (e.g., Strava or Spotify) must be able to call
standard WCSession and Multipeer Connectivity methods to communicate with a non-Apple
wearable just as they do with an Apple Watch.
-Parity in Background Execution: Third-party wearables require the same system-level daemon
benefits afforded to Apple Watch, including the ability to queue data, manage retries, and wake
the companion app upon data arrival.
-Protocol-Level Functional Parity: We require both software API access and the underlying
protocol definitions. To ensure these APIs “just work” for third-party hardware, we seek a
solution where Apple either (a) extends these APIs to support third-party protocol
specifications, or (b) provides the necessary protocol documentation to allow third-party
manufacturers to implement Apple’s protocols on their own devices.
2. Specific Framework Requirements
To achieve functional parity with the Apple Watch “first-party” experience, the following
capabilities must be exposed:
-WatchConnectivity (WCSession): Access to methods including updateApplicationContext(_:)
for state syncing, transferFile(_:metadata:) for background data, and
sendMessage(_:replyHandler:errorHandler:) for interactive messaging.
-Multipeer Connectivity: Access to the framework’s session, advertising, and browsing
capabilities (including MCSession, MCNearbyServiceAdvertiser, and
MCNearbyServiceBrowser) to allow any iOS app to discover and establish sessions with non-
Apple wearables via system-managed discovery.
3. Rebuttal on DMA Scope (The Efficiency Gap)
You stated that concerns regarding "oversized app binaries" and "fragmentation" fall outside
the scope of Article 6(7) DMA. We disagree. These are direct results of an interoperability gap.
The absence of a centralized system service forces developers to bundle redundant SDKs for
every wearable they support, leading to “redundant code taking up storage space,” a factor
explicitly recognized in recital 452(b) of the EC’s specification decision (DMA.100203).
4. Procedural Requests
To move forward effectively, we ask for the following:
-Scope Confirmation: Please confirm which specific APIs, system-level behaviors (e.g.,
background waking), and protocol definitions are included in your Phase 3 implementation
plan.
-Eligibility Assessment: For any portion of our request that you believe is not encompassed by
the EC specification decision—specifically the ability for any iOS app to utilize WCSession or
MCSession to communicate with a non-Apple device via standard protocols—we ask that you
complete a Phase 1 eligibility assessment immediately.
We remain available to discuss these technical details at your earliest convenience.
Kind regards,
Ted
